# Marketing Psychology Audit — Tadnun

*Audited: 2026-03-27*

## 1. Current Psychological Triggers Inventory

### What is already being used, and how effectively.

#### 1.1 Urgency / Fear of Missing Out (FOMO)
**Where used:** Urgency section on the homepage (dark full-bleed block), About page Vision 2030 section.
**Copy:** "While you hesitate, your competitors are going digital." / "In 2030, Morocco will host the World Cup. Millions of visitors will search for businesses online. Companies that aren't visible simply won't exist."
**Rating: 8/10 — Strong.**
The 2030 World Cup countdown is a near-perfect urgency driver for this audience. It is a real, government-backed deadline that every Moroccan business owner understands. The "your competitors are moving" framing adds competitive anxiety. The 93% statistic gives it factual weight.
**What could be stronger:** The urgency section currently sits below the fold, after the sectors section and the stats bar. A visitor who bounces early never sees it. The countdown is also framed in the abstract ("2030") rather than as a concrete number ("1,461 days left"). A live countdown counter would create much sharper temporal urgency.

#### 1.2 Loss Aversion (Pain-First Framing)
**Where used:** Every sector card on the homepage, every sector detail page, the About page founding stories, the sectors list "signs" section.
**Copy examples:** "400,000 MAD export contract is rejected" / "300,000 MAD per year you send to Amsterdam" / "2,000 MAD daily lost to an empty chair"
**Rating: 9/10 — Excellent.**
This is the single strongest psychological lever on the site. Specific MAD figures make abstract losses visceral. The quotes from business owners ("I lost a contract because one farmer didn't write down when he treated his trees") use customer language, which is far more persuasive than company language. Each sector has its own concrete loss figure, which makes it personal.
**What could be stronger:** The loss figures appear primarily in body copy. They should be given more visual weight — larger type, isolated callouts, or comparison frames ("This is what you lose vs. this is what the solution costs"). Currently, the pain is stated but never contrasted against the price of inaction vs. the price of Tadnun, which is where loss aversion truly converts.

#### 1.3 Social Proof (Numbers and Testimonials)
**Where used:** Stats bar (150+ businesses, 8 sectors, 99.9% uptime, 24h response), testimonials on homepage sector cards, testimonials on sector detail pages, "150+ businesses have trusted us" on the contact page.
**Rating: 5/10 — Present but underdeployed.**
The 150+ stat is used, but it lacks emotional weight. There are no company logos, no before/after photos, no video testimonials, no named companies with recognizable businesses. The testimonials are first-name-only with a city, which in the Moroccan market feels anonymous. A Marrakech restaurant owner named "Karim" could be anyone. Social proof works through identification ("someone like me did this") and verification ("I can check this is real"), and both are weak here.
**Specific gaps:**
- No client logos or business photos anywhere on the site
- No case study pages with detailed before/after results
- No Google review ratings or external platform proof
- The "150+" stat is the same on every page — it becomes wallpaper
- No video testimonials (extremely high-trust for tech-hesitant audiences)
- Testimonials are not attributed to verifiable businesses

#### 1.4 Authority (Credibility Signals)
**Where used:** ONSSA, CMI, CNSS, GlobalGAP, CNDP mentioned in integration sections; "Built for Morocco" messaging; "8 sectors covered."
**Rating: 3/10 — Weak.**
Integration mentions create implied authority ("we work with ONSSA") but there are no partnership badges, no "certified by" markers, no media mentions, no team bios, no founder story with credentials, no association memberships, and no "as featured in" section. For a Moroccan audience that is skeptical of digital agencies (many have been burned by freelancers who take money and disappear), authority signals are critical. The site currently asks the visitor to trust Tadnun based on copy alone.

#### 1.5 Simplicity / Cognitive Ease
**Where used:** "If you can use WhatsApp, you can use Tadnun" — repeated across expertise section, FAQ, sector pages. The WhatsApp-first contact page. The 3-step approach (Listen, Build, Grow).
**Rating: 7/10 — Good concept, inconsistent execution.**
The WhatsApp analogy is brilliant for this audience. It is the one digital tool every Moroccan business owner uses. Making the product feel as familiar as WhatsApp lowers the anxiety barrier significantly. The 3-step approach is clear and reduces perceived complexity. However, the site itself is content-heavy — sector detail pages have 8+ sections. The cognitive load of the site contradicts the simplicity message.

#### 1.6 Reciprocity (Give Before You Ask)
**Where used:** "Free 15-minute discovery call," "Free diagnostic," "First month free."
**Rating: 4/10 — Basic.**
The free call is a standard B2B offer. It does not feel like a gift. Reciprocity works best when the visitor receives value before being asked to commit. Currently, the entire site is "read about us, then contact us." There is no free tool, no downloadable guide, no self-service assessment, no free audit. The visitor gives attention but receives nothing tangible in return until they make contact.

## 2. Missing or Underused Principles

### 2.1 Authority: How to Add Credibility Signals

**The problem:** A tech-hesitant Moroccan business owner visiting the site sees no proof that Tadnun is a real, established company rather than a freelancer with a nice website. In a market where many have been burned by unreliable agencies, this is a conversion killer.

**Recommendations (ranked by impact):**

1. **Client logo bar** — Add a horizontal strip of 8-12 client logos (or business names with city) immediately below the hero on the homepage. Even text-only ("Trusted by Cooperative Al Amal, Riad Noor, Cafe Atlas, Clinique Dar Salam...") would help. This is the single highest-impact authority signal missing.

2. **Founder/team section on the About page** — A photo and 2-line bio of the founder. In Moroccan business culture, people buy from people, not companies. The About page tells the company story but not a human story.

3. **Partnership and compliance badges** — If Tadnun is a registered company (RC, IF numbers), mention it. If there are partnerships with CMI, or if the team holds any certifications (Google Partner, Meta Business Partner, Odoo partner), display them. Even "Registered in Morocco — RC Casablanca" adds legitimacy.

4. **Media or press mentions** — If Tadnun has been mentioned by any outlet (even a local blog, a podcast, a chamber of commerce event), create an "As seen in" bar. If not, proactively seek one or two — a guest post on a Moroccan business site, a mention in a CRI newsletter.

5. **Government/institutional signals** — "Member of CGEM" or "Supported by Maroc PME" or "Partner of CRI [region]" would be enormously credible for SME owners who trust institutional backing.

### 2.2 Commitment & Consistency: Micro-Commitments Before the Full Ask

**The problem:** The site jumps from "read about us" to "contact us via WhatsApp or form." For a tech-hesitant audience, this is a large psychological jump. They go from passive browsing to active commitment (revealing their name, phone number, and admitting they need help) with no intermediate steps.

**Recommendations:**

1. **"Is your business ready?" self-assessment quiz** (highest impact) — A 5-question interactive quiz on the sectors page or homepage: "Do you manage inventory on paper? (Yes/No)" "Do customers find you on Google? (Yes/No)" etc. At the end, show a score ("Your digitalization readiness: 2/10") and offer a personalized recommendation. This creates a micro-commitment (answering questions), provides immediate value (a score), and primes the visitor to take the next step. Critically, it also generates a lead even without contact info — if the quiz asks for an email to "send your full report," the visitor has already invested effort and is far more likely to provide it (consistency principle).

2. **Sector-specific "cost of inaction" calculator** — "How much is your restaurant losing? Enter your average covers per day and your no-show rate." This uses the endowment effect (once they see "their" number, it becomes personal) and commitment (they spent 30 seconds with the tool).

3. **"Download your sector guide" lead magnet** — A 2-page PDF per sector: "5 digital tools every Moroccan restaurant needs in 2026." Micro-commitment: give email to receive value. This is lighter than a phone call.

4. **Progressive disclosure on the contact form** — Start with just one field ("What sector are you in?"), then reveal the next field on answer. This reduces perceived effort and uses the foot-in-the-door technique.

### 2.3 Reciprocity: What to Give First

**The problem:** The site asks for trust (contact us) without first demonstrating competence in a tangible way. "Free discovery call" is not reciprocity — it is a sales meeting with a different name.

**Recommendations:**

1. **Free Google Business audit** — "Enter your business name. We'll show you how you appear on Google right now — and what your competitors look like." Automated, instant, and genuinely useful. This is the single best reciprocity play for this audience because (a) Google visibility is a pain point across all sectors, (b) the result is immediately visible and shareable, and (c) it primes the visitor to want to fix what they see.

2. **Sector-specific checklists** — "The 10-point digitalization checklist for Moroccan restaurants" — downloadable without giving an email (true reciprocity). Place a soft CTA at the bottom: "Want help implementing this? Talk to us."

3. **Free WhatsApp templates** — "5 WhatsApp auto-reply templates for Moroccan businesses" — this is brilliantly aligned with the audience's existing tool (WhatsApp) and positions Tadnun as helpful before asking for anything.

4. **Public ROI calculator** — Let visitors calculate their own potential savings. "You spend X MAD/month on Booking.com commissions. With a direct booking site, you'd save Y." The output is the gift; the next step is "Want us to build this?"

### 2.4 Anchoring: How to Frame Custom Pricing

**The problem:** The pricing section shows 299/999/2,999 MAD/month tiers, but these numbers float without context. A business owner has no reference point for whether 999 MAD/month is expensive or cheap. Without anchoring, price-sensitive SME owners will default to "too expensive."

**Recommendations:**

1. **Anchor against the cost of inaction** — Directly above or beside the pricing section, add: "The average Moroccan restaurant loses 180,000 MAD/year to food waste and missed orders. Our Pro plan costs 11,988 MAD/year — a 15x return." This makes the price feel small by contrast. Currently, the pain-point MAD figures and the pricing live in separate sections with no explicit connection between them.

2. **Anchor against alternatives** — "An Odoo integrator charges 50,000-150,000 MAD for setup alone. A freelance developer takes 3-6 months and may disappear. Tadnun: 999 MAD/month, everything included, cancel anytime." This positions the price against much larger numbers.

3. **Anchor against familiar spending** — "Less than the cost of one part-time employee" or "Less than what you spend on paper and ink each month" — relatable anchors for the audience.

4. **Show the "annual savings" not the monthly cost** — Reframe: "2 months free on annual" becomes "Save 5,998 MAD/year on Premium — that's a round-trip to Istanbul." Make the savings concrete and desirable.

### 2.5 Specificity: Where to Add More Concrete Numbers

**The problem:** The site is already good at sector-specific pain figures, but several sections use vague language where specific numbers would be more persuasive.

**Specific locations where numbers should replace words:**

| Current copy | Better version | Principle |
|---|---|---|
| "150+ businesses served" | "153 businesses across 12 cities" | Specificity bias — odd numbers feel more honest |
| "24h max response time" | "Average response: 23 minutes" (this stat exists but is buried on the contact page only) | Specificity + superiority |
| "99.9% guaranteed uptime" | "99.94% uptime over the last 12 months" | Specificity bias |
| "We reply within an hour" (contact page) | "Average first reply: 23 minutes (measured over 1,200 conversations)" | Social proof + specificity |
| "8 sectors covered" | "8 sectors, 37 solution modules, 12 Moroccan cities" | Breadth signaling |
| "150+ businesses have trusted us" (CTA band) | "153 businesses. 8 sectors. 4 years. Your turn." | Temporal authority + specificity |

### 2.6 Social Proof Enhancement: Where to Surface Proof More Prominently

**Recommendations by page:**

**Homepage:**
- Add a client logo bar directly below the hero (before sectors). This is the #1 most-viewed area after the headline. Currently, the proof sits in a stats bar much further down.
- Add a rotating testimonial carousel in the urgency section. The dark background is emotionally charged — proof here would convert fear into action.
- Each sector card on the homepage already has a pain quote. Add the result alongside it: "'We get 40% more customers on weekends.' — Karim, Marrakech" is far more powerful than just the pain.

**Sector pages:**
- Move testimonials above the fold, directly after the hero. Currently, they sit below three pain points — the visitor must scroll past problems to find proof. Proof should follow the problem immediately.
- Add "results" badges next to ROI stats: "Verified result from active client" adds credibility to numbers that might otherwise feel made up.

**Contact page:**
- Add 2-3 micro-testimonials around the WhatsApp CTA. The contact page currently has one testimonial (sector-dependent) and a "150+ businesses" line. The moment of conversion is when proof matters most.
- Add a "What happens after you message us" with a real screenshot of a WhatsApp conversation (anonymized). This reduces uncertainty about what the experience will feel like.

## 4. Moroccan Market Considerations

### 4.1 Trust-Building in a Skeptical Market

Moroccan SME owners have been burned. They have paid freelancers who disappeared, bought SaaS tools they could not use, and heard promises from agencies that were not kept. This creates a "trust deficit" that is the single biggest conversion barrier for Tadnun.

**What the site does well:**
- WhatsApp-first contact eliminates the "corporate barrier" — WhatsApp is personal, immediate, and familiar.
- "No commitment, cancel anytime" directly addresses lock-in anxiety.
- Darija/French language support signals "we are like you, not a foreign company."
- The "we visit your business first" message (approach page) is powerful — it signals investment and seriousness.
- The on-site training promise is the strongest trust builder in the copy: "We don't leave until it works."

**What is missing:**
- **Physical proof of existence.** A Moroccan business owner wants to know: Where is your office? Can I visit you? A physical address (even a co-working space) on the contact page adds significant trust. "Based in Morocco" is vague; "Based in Casablanca, serving all of Morocco" is better; an actual address is best.
- **Named humans.** The entire site is written in the third person ("we"). There is no named individual. In Moroccan business culture, relationships are personal. "Your dedicated contact: [Name], [Photo], [Phone]" on the contact page would transform conversion.
- **Local references.** The site mentions Moroccan cities (Essaouira, Agadir, Marrakech, Casablanca, Rabat, Fes), which is good. Adding regional knowledge signals would strengthen this: "We know the Souss-Massa cooperative landscape. We know the Marrakech tourist season. We understand Casablanca retail." This communicates "we are insiders, not outsiders."
- **Verifiable claims.** "150+ businesses served" should be verifiable. A portfolio page with 10-15 mini case studies (even just name, city, sector, one result) would let skeptical visitors confirm that Tadnun is real.

### 4.2 Role of Personal Relationships

In Morocco, business deals happen through personal connections. The decision to hire Tadnun will often follow this pattern: (1) A friend or peer mentions it, (2) The owner checks the website, (3) The owner talks to a real person via WhatsApp, (4) They meet in person, (5) They sign.

**Implications for the site:**

1. **The website's job is step 2-3, not step 5.** The site does not need to close the deal — it needs to make the visitor comfortable enough to send a WhatsApp message. This means reducing friction to the first message is more important than adding information.

2. **Referral is the most powerful channel.** The site has no referral mechanism. Adding "Know a business owner who needs this? Share this page" or a WhatsApp share button on sector pages would tap into the natural word-of-mouth dynamic.

3. **The WhatsApp conversation IS the sales process.** The chat preview on the contact page is a strong signal. Consider adding more previews that show the tone of conversation: friendly, informal, in Darija. This reduces the anxiety of "what will they say to me?"

4. **Personal connection on the website.** A video introduction from the founder (30 seconds, in Darija/French, filmed in a real business context) would be extraordinarily powerful. Video creates parasocial trust that text cannot match.

### 4.3 Word-of-Mouth Importance

Word-of-mouth is the primary marketing channel for Moroccan SMEs. An owner trusts their cousin's recommendation more than any website. The site should be designed to facilitate and amplify word-of-mouth.

**Recommendations:**

1. **Make the site shareable.** Every sector page should have a WhatsApp share button: "Share this with a business owner who needs it." WhatsApp sharing is the Moroccan equivalent of email forwarding.

2. **Create "share-worthy" content.** The sector-specific pain quotes ("Booking takes 300,000 MAD per year from me") are inherently shareable — they are emotional, specific, and relatable. Package them as standalone shareable images or cards.

3. **Referral program.** "Refer a business. If they sign up, you both get your first month free." This does not need to be complex — even an informal "mention your friend's name" mechanism works in this market.

4. **Testimonials as referral proof.** When a business owner sees "Karim, restaurant owner, Marrakech" succeeded with Tadnun, they may know Karim — or someone who knows Karim. With permission, adding business names (not just first names) to testimonials would enable this chain.

### 4.4 Price Sensitivity and Value Framing

Moroccan SME owners are not cheap — they are cautious. They spend significant amounts on rent, inventory, and employees without hesitation. What they resist is spending on things they cannot see or touch. "Software" feels intangible. "A person who comes to my business and fixes my problems" feels real.

**How the site handles this today:**
- Pricing tiers are clear (299/999/2,999 MAD/month).
- "No hidden fees" and "first month free" reduce risk.
- FAQ addresses affordability.

**What should change:**

1. **Frame the price as a fraction of the loss.** This is the most critical change. Currently, the pricing section and the pain-point sections are disconnected. They should be visually and narratively linked. Example: Show the pain figure (300,000 MAD/year in Booking commissions) alongside the solution cost (999 MAD/month = 11,988 MAD/year) and the savings (288,012 MAD/year). The ratio is the conversion tool.

2. **Monthly pricing should show daily cost.** "999 MAD/month" feels like a recurring expense. "33 MAD/day — less than a Deliveroo order" feels trivial. For the Starter plan: "10 MAD/day — less than a coffee."

3. **"Investment" not "cost."** The copy already avoids the word "cost" in most places. Ensure it is never used in the pricing section. "Your investment" or "Your plan" frames the money as going somewhere productive, not disappearing.

4. **Risk reversal.** "First month free" is good. Strengthen it: "Try for 30 days. If you don't see results, you pay nothing. Not convinced? We'll help you export your data and part ways. No penalty, no guilt." The longer and more detailed the guarantee, the more credible it feels.

5. **Payment flexibility.** For this audience, "Pay via bank transfer or CMI card" is important — both are mentioned. Consider adding "Pay per quarter" as a middle option between monthly (feels risky to commit) and annual (feels like a big upfront cost). The quarterly option may actually be the preferred choice for Moroccan SME owners who plan in seasonal cycles.

## 6. Psychological Profile of the Target Visitor

Understanding who is on the other side of the screen:

**Mental state when arriving:** Curious but skeptical. They have heard about digitalization, may have been told by a friend or seen a competitor's success. They are not actively searching for "digital transformation agency" — they are searching for a solution to a specific pain ("how to get on Google Maps" or "restaurant management software Morocco").

**Primary fears:**
1. "They will take my money and nothing will change" (agency distrust)
2. "My staff cannot use technology" (competence anxiety)
3. "I will be locked into something I don't understand" (control anxiety)
4. "This is too expensive for a business my size" (price anxiety)
5. "This is not for businesses like mine" (relevance anxiety)

**Decision-making pattern:** Slow, relational, consensus-based. The owner will browse the site, maybe twice, over days. They will ask a friend. They will WhatsApp first, then call, then meet in person. The website's role is to survive the "is this legitimate?" test and provide enough material that the owner can show their partner or spouse: "Look at this — they help restaurants like ours."

**What converts them:** Not features, not technology descriptions, not process diagrams. What converts them is seeing someone like them — same city, same sector, same size — who succeeded. The single most powerful element on the site is a testimonial from a business owner in their sector, in their region, with a specific result. Everything else is supporting material.
